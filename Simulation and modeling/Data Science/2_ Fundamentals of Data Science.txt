    1.1 Introduction to Data Science
******************************************

        Data Science is the process of collecting, cleaning, organizing,
        analyzing, and interpreting data to find useful information and
        support decision making.

        In simple words:
        ---------------------
        Data Science means using data to understand what happened,
        why it happened, what may happen next, and what action should be taken.



        Basic idea of Data Science
        -----------------------------
            Data
                Raw facts or values
                Examples:
                    Student marks
                    Shop sales
                    Temperature records
                    Website visitors


            Science
                Systematic study and analysis
                Using logic, maths, statistics, and computing


            Data Science
                Combines:
                    Mathematics
                    Statistics
                    Programming
                    Domain knowledge
                    Communication skills


        Main goals of Data Science
            Convert raw data into meaningful information
            Find patterns
            Make predictions
            Support better decisions


        Simple real-life example

            Suppose a college has student marks:
                Ram      45
                Sita     78
                Hari     60
                Gita     90
                Nabin    30

            Raw data only shows numbers.
                Data Science helps answer:
                    What is the average mark?
                    Who is at risk of failing?
                    How many students scored above 60?
                    Can next semester's result be predicted?



        Types of questions in Data Science
        -------------------------------------
            Descriptive
                What happened?
                Example:
                    Average sales last month was Rs. 50000

            Diagnostic
                Why did it happen?
                Example:
                    Sales dropped because rainy weather reduced customers

            Predictive
                What might happen next?
                Example:
                    Next month sales may increase by 10 percent

            Prescriptive
                What should be done?
                Example:
                    Offer discount on weekends to increase sales




        Core steps in Data Science
        -----------------------------
            1. Collect data
            2. Clean data
            3. Explore data
            4. Analyze data
            5. Build model
            6. Interpret results
            7. Communicate findings




        Example of complete flow
        -------------------------
            Problem:
                A shop owner wants to know which product sells best

            Data collection:
                Rice      20 units
                Soap      35 units
                Oil       15 units
                Sugar     25 units

            Cleaning:
                Remove duplicate entries
                Fix missing values

            Analysis:
                Soap has highest sales

            Decision:
                Keep more soap stock




        Important skills in Data Science
        -----------------------------------
            Mathematics
                Algebra
                Probability
                Linear algebra

            Statistics
                Mean
                Median
                Variance
                Correlation

            Programming
                Python
                R
                SQL

            Visualization
                Charts
                Graphs
                Dashboards

            Communication
                Explaining results in simple language




        Difference between Data and Information
        ---------------------------------------
            Data
                Unprocessed facts
                Example:
                    12, 15, 18, 20, 22

            Information
                Meaningful result after analysis
                Example:
                    Average temperature is 17.4 degree





        Difference between Data Science and Statistics
        -------------------------------------------------
            Statistics focuses more on analysis and inference
            Data Science includes statistics plus programming,
            data handling, and machine learning




        Difference between Data Science and Data Analytics
        ----------------------------------------------------    
        https://medium.com/analytics-vidhya/introduction-to-data-science-28deb32878e7

            Data Analytics often focuses on understanding past data
            Data Science includes analytics plus prediction and model building





        Mini example with words data
        --------------------------------
            Text data:
                apple
                banana
                apple
                mango
                apple
                banana

            Question:
                Which word appears most?

            Count:
                apple     3
                banana    2
                mango     1

            Conclusion:
                Most common word is apple

            This is also a simple form of Data Science.

******************************************
    1.3 Limitations of Data Science
******************************************
        Although Data Science is powerful, it has some limitations.

        1.3.1 Bad Data Gives Bad Result
        -------------------------------------
            If input data is wrong, output will also be wrong

            Example:
                Suppose student marks are recorded as:
                    Ram     450 instead of 45

            Then:
                Average will be wrong
                Any analysis based on this will be misleading

            This is often called:
                Garbage In, Garbage Out (GIGO)




        1.3.2 Missing or Incomplete Data
        -------------------------------------
            Sometimes data is not fully available

            Example:
                Age     Salary
                25      30000
                ?       40000
                30      ?

            Problem:
                Analysis becomes difficult
                Prediction may be less accurate



        1.3.3 Dependence on Domain Knowledge
        -------------------------------------
            Data alone is not enough
            Need understanding of the field

            Example:
                Medical data must be interpreted with help of health experts



        1.3.4 Bias in Data
        -------------------------------------
            If data is biased, the result will also be biased

            Example:
                If a survey is taken only from city people,
                the result cannot represent rural people

            Common biases:
                Sampling bias
                Measurement bias
                Historical bias



        1.3.5 Privacy and Security Issues
        -------------------------------------
            Personal data must be protected

            Example:
                Bank account details
                Medical records
                Phone location data

            Risks:
                Unauthorized access
                Misuse of personal information




        1.3.6 Cannot Guarantee Perfect Prediction
        -------------------------------------
            Models predict based on patterns in past data
            Future may change

            Example:
                A sales model based on normal days may fail during
                pandemic or festival time



        1.3.7 High Cost and Resource Requirement
        -------------------------------------
            Large projects need:
                Skilled people
                Powerful computers
                Storage
                Time

            Example:
                Training large deep learning models can be expensive




        1.3.8 Difficult to Explain Some Models
        -------------------------------------
            Some models are complex and hard to interpret

            Example:
                Deep neural networks may give high accuracy
                but users may not understand why the decision was made



        1.3.9 Overfitting
        -------------------------------------
            A model may learn training data too well
            Then it fails on new data

            Example:
                A student result model works perfectly on old data
                but gives poor result for new students




        1.3.10 Ethical Problems
        -------------------------------------
            Data Science can be used unfairly

            Examples:
                Unfair hiring system
                Biased loan approval
                Surveillance without consent



        1.3.11 Correlation is not Causation
        -------------------------------------
            Two variables may move together
            but one does not necessarily cause the other

            Example:
                Ice cream sales and drowning cases may both increase in summer
                This does not mean ice cream causes drowning




        1.3.12 Constant Change in Tools and Technology
        -------------------------------------
            Tools change fast
            Learners must keep updating skills

            Example:
                New frameworks, libraries, and platforms appear regularly





        How to reduce limitations
        -------------------------------------
            Use clean and reliable data
            Check for bias
            Protect privacy
            Validate models
            Use domain experts
            Interpret results carefully
            Update skills regularly



        Simple maths example of limitation

            Data:
                2, 4, 6, 8, 100

            Average:
                (2 + 4 + 6 + 8 + 100) / 5
                120 / 5
                24

            Problem:
                Average 24 does not represent most values well
                because 100 is an outlier

            Lesson:
                Data must be checked before analysis





        Simple words example of limitation

            Comments:
                good
                good
                good
                bad

            System may say:
                Mostly positive

            But if "bad" comment is about a serious safety problem,
            simple counting may miss the real issue

            Business
            Education

            Agriculture
            Many other fields

        Limitations include:
            Bad data
            Bias
            Privacy issues
            High cost
            Imperfect predictions

******************************************
1.4 Tools in Data Science
******************************************
    Data Science uses different tools for different types of work.
    Some tools are better for statistics.
    Some are better for programming.
    Some are better for visualization and reporting.


    Main idea:
        No single tool does everything best.
        A data scientist chooses tools based on the problem,
        the size of data, the type of analysis, and the final output needed.


    Common use of tools:
        R and RStudio
            Best known for statistics, data analysis, and academic work

        Python, Pandas, Jupyter
            Best known for programming, machine learning, and flexible data workflows

        Excel, Tableau, PowerBI
            Best known for spreadsheet work, dashboards, and business reporting




******************************************
    1.4.1 R and RStudio
******************************************
        R
            R is a programming language mainly used for statistics,
            data analysis, and visualization.


        RStudio
            RStudio is an environment or software interface used to write
            and run R code more easily.


        Simple meaning:
            R is the language
            RStudio is the workspace used to work with R



        Why R is used in Data Science
            Strong in statistics
            Good for data visualization
            Useful for research and academic analysis
            Has many statistical packages
            Helpful for hypothesis testing and modeling



        Common tasks done in R
      ------------------------------------
            Data cleaning
            Descriptive statistics
            Regression analysis
            Statistical testing
            Data visualization
            Reporting



        Important packages in R
      ------------------------------------
            ggplot2
                Used for graphs and plots

            dplyr
                Used for data manipulation

            tidyr
                Used for reshaping and cleaning data

            caret
                Used for machine learning

            shiny
                Used for interactive web applications and dashboards






        Example using R
      ------------------------------------
            Suppose we have student marks:
                45, 78, 60, 90, 30

            In R, we can calculate average, maximum, and minimum.

            Example idea:
                marks = c(45, 78, 60, 90, 30)

            Then:
                mean(marks) gives average
                max(marks) gives highest mark
                min(marks) gives lowest mark




        Example of use in real life
      ------------------------------------
            A researcher wants to test whether male and female students
            have different average marks.
            R is useful because it provides statistical tests easily.

        Strengths of R
      ------------------------------------
            Excellent for statistics
            Very strong for visualization
            Large collection of packages
            Good for research and academic work





        Limitations of R
      ------------------------------------
            Less beginner-friendly for some users
            Can be slower for very large production systems
            Not always the first choice for software development




        When to use R
      ------------------------------------
            When the work is statistics-heavy
            When doing research
            When making detailed graphs
            When writing analytical reports

        Common tasks done in Python
      ------------------------------------
            Data cleaning
            Data analysis
            Visualization
            Machine learning
            Web scraping
            Automation

            Natural language processing






        Important Python libraries

            Test trends
            Create detailed graphs

        Using Python
            Clean data
            Predict next month's sales
            Build machine learning model

        Using Tableau or PowerBI
            Show sales dashboard for manager
            Add filters by month, city, and product

        So, one problem can use multiple tools at different stages.









    Final understanding
      ------------------------------------
        Data Science tools help at different steps:
            Collecting data

            Visualizing data

1.5 Data Science Lifecycle
******************************************
    Data Science lifecycle means the step-by-step process followed in a
    data science project from the beginning of a problem to the final solution.

    Simple meaning:
        It is a roadmap that helps a data scientist move from
        raw data to useful decision or prediction.

    Why lifecycle is important
        It gives structure to the work
        It reduces confusion
        It helps teams work in order
        It makes projects easier to manage
        It improves the chance of getting useful results

    In real life, a data science project does not start directly with coding.
    It usually starts with understanding the problem.

    General idea of a Data Science lifecycle
        1. Understand the problem
        2. Collect data
        3. Clean and prepare data
        4. Explore and analyze data
        5. Build model
        6. Evaluate results
        7. Communicate and use the result

    Example of lifecycle in a simple problem

        Problem:
            A school wants to predict which students may fail the final exam.

        Step 1
            Understand the problem
            Goal is to identify weak students early

        Step 2
            Collect data
            Attendance, marks, assignments, class tests

        Step 3
            Prepare data
            Remove missing values
            Correct wrong entries

        Step 4
            Explore data
            Check patterns
            Compare attendance and marks

        Step 5
            Build model
            Use classification model

        Step 6
            Evaluate
            Check whether prediction is accurate

        Step 7
            Use result
            Provide extra support to at-risk students

    There are different lifecycle models used in Data Science.
    Three common ones are:
        CRISP-DM
        OSEMN
        TDSP






    1.5.1 CRISP-DM
******************************************
        Full form:
            Cross Industry Standard Process for Data Mining

        CRISP-DM is one of the most popular and widely used lifecycle models.
        It is called cross-industry because it can be used in many fields such as:
            Business
            Education
            Healthcare
            Banking
            Government

        Main phases of CRISP-DM
            1. Business Understanding
            2. Data Understanding
            3. Data Preparation
            4. Modeling
            5. Evaluation
            6. Deployment

        1. Business Understanding

            First understand the real problem.

            Questions asked:
                What is the goal?
                Why is this project needed?
                What decision should be supported?
                What does success mean?

            Example:
                A shop wants to increase sales.
                The real question may be:
                    Which products should be promoted?

            This phase is very important because a wrong problem definition
            can lead to a useless project.

        2. Data Understanding

            In this step, we collect and study the data.

            Tasks:
                Gather available data
                Identify data sources
                Look at data shape and type
                Find errors, missing values, unusual patterns

            Example:
                Product sales data may include:
                    Product name
                    Quantity sold
                    Date
                    Price
                    Customer location

            Questions:
                Is data enough?
                Is data reliable?
                Are there missing records?

        3. Data Preparation

            This step prepares data for analysis or modeling.

            Tasks:
                Clean data
                Handle missing values
                Remove duplicates
                Convert formats
                Select useful columns
                Create new features

            Example:
                Date may be converted into:
                    Day
                    Month
                    Season

                Sales amount may be calculated as:
                    Quantity multiplied by price

            This step often takes the most time in real projects.

        4. Modeling

            In this step, models are built.

            Tasks:
                Choose algorithm
                Train model
                Tune parameters
                Compare different models

            Example:
                For sales prediction, use:
                    Linear regression
                For pass or fail prediction, use:
                    Logistic regression
                    Decision tree

        5. Evaluation

            Check whether the model really solves the original problem.

            Questions:
                Is the model accurate enough?
                Does it answer the business problem?
                Are the results meaningful?

            Example:
                A model may be mathematically accurate,
                but if it is too difficult to use in practice,
                it may not be useful.

        6. Deployment

            Deployment means putting the model or result into actual use.

            This may include:
                Creating a dashboard
                Generating a report
                Building a software tool
                Integrating model into a website or app

            Example:
                A student-risk model may be used by teachers
                to identify weak students every month.

        Important feature of CRISP-DM

            CRISP-DM is not always linear.
            Sometimes we go back.

            Example:
                If model performance is poor,
                we may return to Data Preparation
                or even Data Understanding.

        Simple CRISP-DM example with marks data

            Goal:
                Predict student pass or fail

            Business Understanding
                School wants early warning system

            Data Understanding
                Collect attendance, class test marks, homework

            Data Preparation
                Remove missing rows
                Convert pass/fail into labels

            Modeling
                Train classification model

            Evaluation
                Check accuracy

            Deployment
                Give monthly report to teachers

        Advantages of CRISP-DM
            Clear and structured
            Easy to understand
            Works in many industries
            Focuses on real problem solving

        Limitation of CRISP-DM
            It is broad, so teams still need practical tools and methods
            It does not say exactly which algorithm to use

1.6 Statistics and Probability
******************************************
    Statistics and probability are two very important foundations of Data Science.

    Simple meaning:
        Probability helps us measure uncertainty.
        Statistics helps us collect, summarize, analyze, and interpret data.

    Why they are important in Data Science
        Real-world data is often uncertain
        Decisions are often made based on incomplete information
        Predictions are never fully certain
        We need mathematical tools to describe data and uncertainty

    Example:
        If a student usually scores between 55 and 75,
        we may want to estimate the chance that the student scores above 60.

        This kind of question uses probability.

        If we have marks of 100 students and want to find:
            average mark
            highest mark
            spread of marks
        this uses statistics.

    Difference between Statistics and Probability

        Probability
            Starts with a known model or known rules
            Predicts what may happen

            Example:
                A fair coin has 2 possible outcomes
                Probability of head = 1/2

        Statistics
            Starts with observed data
            Tries to understand what the data says

            Example:
                Out of 50 students, 35 passed
                Use data to understand pass rate

    In Data Science, both are used together.

        Example:
            Statistics helps summarize past sales
            Probability helps predict future sales








    1.6.1 Probability Distributions
******************************************
        A probability distribution shows how probabilities are assigned
        to possible values of a random variable.

        Random variable
            A variable whose value depends on chance

        Examples:
            Number of heads in 3 coin tosses
            Marks obtained by a student
            Daily sales of a shop
            Number of customers visiting a store

        Simple meaning:
            A probability distribution tells us:
                what values are possible
                how likely each value is

        Example with coin tosses

            Suppose we toss 2 coins.
            Let X = number of heads.

            Possible values of X:
                0
                1
                2

            Their probabilities:
                P(X = 0) = 1/4
                P(X = 1) = 2/4
                P(X = 2) = 1/4

            This is a probability distribution.

        Why distributions matter in Data Science
            They help describe uncertain outcomes
            They are used in modeling
            They help in simulation
            They help in machine learning and statistical inference

        Types of probability distributions

            Discrete distributions
                Used when values are countable

                Examples:
                    Number of students absent
                    Number of calls received
                    Number of defective items

            Continuous distributions
                Used when values can take any value in an interval

                Examples:
                    Height
                    Weight
                    Temperature
                    Time

        Common examples of distributions

            Bernoulli distribution
                Used when there are only 2 outcomes

                Example:
                    Pass or fail
                    Yes or no
                    Head or tail

                If success probability = p
                then failure probability = 1 - p

            Binomial distribution
                Used when:
                    there are fixed number of trials
                    each trial has two outcomes
                    trials are independent
                    probability of success remains same

                Example:
                    Toss a coin 5 times
                    Find probability of getting exactly 3 heads

                If X = number of heads in 5 tosses,
                then X follows binomial distribution.

            Normal distribution
                One of the most important continuous distributions

                Shape:
                    Bell-shaped
                    Symmetric around the mean

                Example:
                    Heights of students often follow approximately normal distribution

                If average height = 160 cm,
                most students will be near 160,
                and fewer students will be very short or very tall.

            Uniform distribution
                All values in a range are equally likely

                Example:
                    A random number between 0 and 1

            Poisson distribution
                Used for counting how many times an event happens
                in a fixed interval

                Example:
                    Number of customers entering a shop in one hour
                    Number of emails received in one minute

        Example using marks data

            Suppose marks are grouped like this:
                40 to 49     5 students
                50 to 59    10 students
                60 to 69    20 students
                70 to 79    10 students
                80 to 89     5 students

            This tells us the distribution of marks.
            We can see that most students are in the 60 to 69 range.

        Key ideas in distributions

            Mean
                Center of the data

            Variance
                Spread of the data

            Standard deviation
                Average variation from mean

            Shape
                Symmetric or skewed

        Simple maths example

            Let X be the result of rolling a fair die.

            Possible values:
                1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6

            Probability distribution:
                P(X = 1) = 1/6
                P(X = 2) = 1/6
                P(X = 3) = 1/6
                P(X = 4) = 1/6
                P(X = 5) = 1/6
                P(X = 6) = 1/6

            This is a uniform discrete distribution.

        Why understanding distributions helps
            Helps choose correct model
            Helps detect unusual values
            Helps understand whether data is balanced or not
            Helps in forecasting and decision making







    1.6.2 Compound Events
******************************************
        A compound event is an event made by combining two or more simple events.

        Simple event
            An event involving one condition

            Example:
                Getting a head in one toss

        Compound event
            An event involving more than one condition

            Example:
                Getting a head and then a tail
                Drawing a red card or a king

        Common ways to form compound events

            AND
                Both events happen

            OR
                At least one event happens

            NOT
                Event does not happen

        Example with die

            Let:
                A = getting an even number
                B = getting a number greater than 4

            Sample space:
                1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6

            Event A:
                2, 4, 6

            Event B:
                5, 6

            A AND B:
                only 6

            A OR B:
                2, 4, 5, 6

        Compound event with coin toss

            Toss 2 coins

            Sample space:
                HH
                HT
                TH
                TT

            Event:
                at least one head

            Favorable outcomes:
                HH
                HT
                TH

            Probability:
                3/4

        Rules used in compound events

            Addition rule for OR

                P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)

            Why subtract P(A and B)?
                Because it gets counted twice when we add P(A) and P(B)

        Example using cards

            From a standard deck of 52 cards:
                A = drawing a king
                B = drawing a heart

            P(A) = 4/52
            P(B) = 13/52
            P(A and B) = 1/52

            So:
                P(A or B)
                = 4/52 + 13/52 - 1/52
                = 16/52
                = 4/13

        Multiplication rule for AND

            For some cases:
                P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

            This is especially true when A and B are independent.

        Example with two coin tosses

            A = first toss is head
            B = second toss is head

            P(A) = 1/2
            P(B) = 1/2

            So:
                P(A and B) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4

            This matches the result for HH.

        Example from student data

            Suppose:
                P(student passes maths) = 0.8
                P(student passes science) = 0.7

            If we assume independence:
                P(pass both)
                = 0.8 × 0.7
                = 0.56

            So there is 56 percent chance of passing both.

        Why compound events matter in Data Science
            Real-world decisions often involve multiple conditions

            Example:
                Customer buys phone and charger
                Patient has fever and cough
                Student has low attendance and low marks

            These are compound conditions,
            and probability helps evaluate them.


    1.6.3 Independence
******************************************
        Two events are independent if the happening of one event
        does not affect the probability of the other event.

        Simple meaning:
            Knowing event A happened does not change event B.

        Example:
            Toss a coin and roll a die

            Event A:
                coin shows head

            Event B:
                die shows 4

            These are independent because the coin result
            does not affect the die result.

        Mathematical condition for independence

            Events A and B are independent if:

                P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

        Example with coin and die

            P(A) = 1/2
            P(B) = 1/6

            If independent:
                P(A and B)
                = 1/2 × 1/6
                = 1/12

        Another example with two coin tosses

            A = first toss is head
            B = second toss is tail

            P(A) = 1/2
            P(B) = 1/2

            P(A and B) = 1/4

            Since:
                1/4 = 1/2 × 1/2

            A and B are independent.

        Example of dependent events

            Suppose a bag has:
                3 red balls
                2 blue balls

            Draw one ball, then draw another without replacement.

            Event A:
                first ball is red

            Event B:
                second ball is red

            These are dependent because the first draw changes the bag.

        Check the probabilities

            P(A) = 3/5

            If first ball was red,
            then remaining red balls = 2
            remaining total balls = 4

            So:
                P(B given A) = 2/4 = 1/2

            Since the probability changed,
            the events are dependent.

        Conditional probability idea

            If two events are dependent,
            we often use:

                P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B given A)

            Here:
                P(B given A) means probability of B after A has occurred

        Example from daily life

            Independent events
                Rain tomorrow
                A student getting 70 in maths today

                These usually do not affect each other.

            Dependent events
                Customer buys laptop
                Customer buys laptop bag

                These may be dependent,
                because buying laptop can increase chance of buying bag.

        Example from data science

            Suppose:
                Event A = a person clicked an online ad
                Event B = the person bought the product

            These may not be independent,
            because clicking the ad may affect buying behavior.

            So in data science,
            checking independence helps understand relationships in data.

        Importance of independence in Data Science
            Used in probability models
            Used in Naive Bayes classifier
            Used in statistical assumptions
            Helps simplify calculations
            Helps decide whether variables affect each other

        Important note

            Independent does not mean unrelated in every practical sense,
            but in probability it means one event does not change
            the chance of the other.

        Common confusion

            Mutually exclusive and independent are different.

            Mutually exclusive
                Two events cannot happen together

                Example:
                    On one die roll,
                    getting 2 and getting 5 cannot happen together

            Independent
                One event does not affect the other

            Important:
                Mutually exclusive events with non-zero probability
                are usually not independent.

        Why?
            If one happens, the other cannot happen.
            So clearly one affects the other.

        Simple example

            On one die roll:
                A = getting 2
                B = getting 5

            They are mutually exclusive
            because both cannot happen together.

            But they are not independent,
            because if A happens, B becomes impossible.

        Simple words example

            Event A:
                A review contains the word "excellent"

            Event B:
                The review is positive

            These may be dependent,
            because seeing "excellent" increases the chance
            that the review is positive.

        Simple maths summary

            Independent:
                P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)

            Dependent:
                P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B given A)

        Quick example comparison

            Independent case
                Coin toss and die roll

            Dependent case
                Two card draws without replacement

        Final understanding

            Probability distributions
                Tell us how likely different values are

            Compound events
                Combine more than one event using AND or OR

            Independence
                Means one event does not affect another

            These are basic but very important ideas
            for understanding uncertainty in Data Science.


    Quick Revision

        Statistics
            Study of data

        Probability
            Study of uncertainty

        Probability distribution
            Shows possible values and their probabilities

        Compound event
            Combination of simple events

        Independence
            One event does not affect another

        Key formulas

            P(A or B)
                = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)

            If independent:
                P(A and B)
                = P(A) × P(B)

            If dependent:
                P(A and B)
                = P(A) × P(B given A)

1.7 Statistics Measures
******************************************
    Statistics measures are numerical values used to summarize data.
    They help us understand the main features of a dataset quickly.

    Simple meaning:
        Instead of looking at all data values one by one,
        statistics measures give us a short summary.

    Example:
        Marks of 5 students:
            40, 50, 60, 70, 80

        From these numbers we may want to know:
            What is the center of the data?
            How spread out is the data?
            Are the values close together or far apart?

    Main groups of statistics measures
        Centrality measures
            Tell us where the center of data lies

        Variability measures
            Tell us how spread out the data is

    Why these measures matter in Data Science
        They help summarize raw data
        They help compare two datasets
        They help detect unusual patterns
        They help prepare data before modeling
        They support decision making


    1.7.1 Centrality Measures
******************************************
        Centrality measures are also called measures of central tendency.

        They tell us the typical or central value of a dataset.

        The three most common centrality measures are:
            Mean
            Median
            Mode

        A. Mean

            Mean is the arithmetic average.

            Formula:
                Mean = Sum of all values / Number of values

            Example:
                Data:
                    10, 20, 30, 40, 50

                Sum:
                    10 + 20 + 30 + 40 + 50 = 150

                Number of values:
                    5

                Mean:
                    150 / 5 = 30

            So the mean is 30.

            Meaning:
                If all values were balanced equally,
                the center would be 30.

            Example with student marks:
                45, 55, 60, 70, 80

                Sum:
                    45 + 55 + 60 + 70 + 80 = 310

                Mean:
                    310 / 5 = 62

            So average mark is 62.

            Strength of mean
                Easy to calculate
                Uses all data values
                Very common in analysis

            Limitation of mean
                Affected by outliers

            Example:
                Data:
                    10, 12, 14, 16, 100

                Sum:
                    152

                Mean:
                    152 / 5 = 30.4

                Problem:
                    Most values are near 10 to 16,
                    but mean became 30.4 because of 100

                So mean can be misleading when there are extreme values.

        B. Median

            Median is the middle value when data is arranged in order.

            Steps:
                Arrange data in ascending or descending order
                Find the middle value

            Example:
                Data:
                    12, 18, 20, 25, 30

                Middle value:
                    20

                So median = 20

            If there is even number of values,
            median is the average of the two middle values.

            Example:
                Data:
                    10, 20, 30, 40

                Middle values:
                    20 and 30

                Median:
                    (20 + 30) / 2 = 25

            Example with outlier:
                Data:
                    10, 12, 14, 16, 100

                Ordered data:
                    10, 12, 14, 16, 100

                Median:
                    14

            Notice:
                Mean was 30.4
                Median is 14

            Here median represents the center better than mean.

            Strength of median
                Not strongly affected by outliers
                Useful for skewed data

            Limitation of median
                Does not use all values directly like mean

        C. Mode

            Mode is the most frequently occurring value.

            Example:
                Data:
                    2, 3, 3, 5, 7

                Mode:
                    3

            Example with words:
                Words:
                    apple, banana, apple, mango, apple

                Mode:
                    apple

            A dataset may have:
                One mode
                    Unimodal

                Two modes
                    Bimodal

                More than two modes
                    Multimodal

                No repeated value
                    No mode

            Example of bimodal data:
                2, 2, 4, 4, 5, 6

                Modes:
                    2 and 4

            Strength of mode
                Easy to understand
                Works for numbers and categories

            Limitation of mode
                May not exist clearly
                May not represent full data well

        Comparison of mean, median, and mode

            Mean
                Best when data is fairly balanced
                Uses all values
                Sensitive to outliers

            Median
                Best when data is skewed or has outliers
                More robust

            Mode
                Best for most common value
                Useful for categorical data

        Example comparison

            Data:
                5, 6, 7, 8, 50

            Mean:
                (5 + 6 + 7 + 8 + 50) / 5
                = 76 / 5
                = 15.2

            Median:
                7

            Mode:
                No mode

            Interpretation:
                Mean is pulled upward by 50
                Median gives more realistic center

        Example with category data

            Favorite subject of students:
                Math
                Science
                Math
                English
                Math
                Science

            Mode:
                Math

            Here mean and median cannot be used meaningfully,
            but mode can be used.

        When to use which

            Use mean
                When data is numerical and not highly skewed

            Use median
                When data has outliers or is skewed

            Use mode
                When most common value is important
                especially in categorical data











    1.7.2 Variability Measures
******************************************
        Variability measures show how spread out the data values are.

        Simple meaning:
            They tell us whether values are close together or far apart.

        Example:
            Dataset A:
                48, 49, 50, 51, 52

            Dataset B:
                10, 30, 50, 70, 90

            Both have mean = 50,
            but dataset B is much more spread out.

        So mean alone is not enough.
        We also need variability measures.

        Common variability measures
            Range
            Interquartile Range
            Variance
            Standard Deviation

        A. Range

            Range = Maximum value - Minimum value

            Example:
                Data:
                    10, 20, 30, 40, 50

                Maximum:
                    50

                Minimum:
                    10

                Range:
                    50 - 10 = 40

            Interpretation:
                The data spreads across 40 units

            Strength of range
                Very easy to calculate

            Limitation of range
                Depends only on two values
                Affected by outliers

        B. Interquartile Range

            Interquartile Range is often called IQR.

            It measures the spread of the middle 50 percent of data.

            Formula:
                IQR = Q3 - Q1

            Where:
                Q1 = first quartile
                Q3 = third quartile

            Example:
                Data:
                    10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80

                Lower half:
                    10, 20, 30, 40

                Upper half:
                    50, 60, 70, 80

                Q1:
                    median of lower half
                    = (20 + 30) / 2 = 25

                Q3:
                    median of upper half
                    = (60 + 70) / 2 = 65

                IQR:
                    65 - 25 = 40

            Meaning:
                The middle half of values is spread across 40 units

            Strength of IQR
                Not strongly affected by outliers
                Good for skewed data

        C. Variance

            Variance measures how far data values are from the mean on average.

            Simple idea:
                First find the mean
                Then see how much each value differs from the mean
                Square the differences
                Find the average of those squared differences

            Variance is discussed more deeply in section 1.7.3

        D. Standard Deviation

            Standard deviation is the square root of variance.

            It shows spread in the same unit as the original data.

            Example:
                If marks are measured in points,
                standard deviation is also in points

            This makes it easier to interpret than variance.

            General meaning:
                Small standard deviation
                    values are close to mean

                Large standard deviation
                    values are far from mean

        Example of spread

            Dataset A:
                49, 50, 51

            Dataset B:
                20, 50, 80

            Both have mean = 50

            But:
                Dataset A has low variability
                Dataset B has high variability

        Why variability matters in Data Science
            Helps compare consistency
            Helps detect outliers
            Helps judge reliability of averages
            Helps understand risk and uncertainty

        Example:
            Two students both have average mark 60

            Student A marks:
                58, 60, 62

            Student B marks:
                20, 60, 100

            Same mean,
            but student B is much more inconsistent

            Variability shows this difference.











    1.7.3 Interpreting Variance
******************************************
        Variance is one of the most important measures of spread.

        Definition:
            Variance tells us how much the values vary around the mean.

        Simple meaning:
            It shows whether the data values stay near the average
            or move far away from it.

        Steps to calculate variance

            Step 1
                Find the mean

            Step 2
                Subtract the mean from each value

            Step 3
                Square each difference

            Step 4
                Find the average of the squared differences

        Example

            Data:
                2, 4, 6

            Step 1: Mean
                (2 + 4 + 6) / 3
                = 12 / 3
                = 4

            Step 2: Differences from mean
                2 - 4 = -2
                4 - 4 = 0
                6 - 4 = 2

            Step 3: Squared differences
                (-2)^2 = 4
                0^2 = 0
                2^2 = 4

            Step 4: Average of squared differences
                (4 + 0 + 4) / 3
                = 8 / 3
                = 2.67 approximately

            So variance is 2.67

        Another example

            Data:
                10, 10, 10, 10

            Mean:
                10

            Differences from mean:
                0, 0, 0, 0

            Squared differences:
                0, 0, 0, 0

            Variance:
                0

            Interpretation:
                No spread at all
                All values are exactly the same

        Example with larger spread

            Data:
                0, 10, 20

            Mean:
                (0 + 10 + 20) / 3 = 10

            Differences:
                -10, 0, 10

            Squared differences:
                100, 0, 100

            Variance:
                (100 + 0 + 100) / 3
                = 200 / 3
                = 66.67 approximately

            Interpretation:
                This dataset has much more spread than 2, 4, 6

        Meaning of low variance

            Low variance means:
                Values are close to the mean
                Data is more consistent
                Less fluctuation

            Example:
                Daily temperatures:
                    29, 30, 31, 30, 29

            These values are close together,
            so variance is low.

        Meaning of high variance

            High variance means:
                Values are spread out
                Data is less consistent
                More fluctuation

            Example:
                Daily temperatures:
                    10, 20, 35, 25, 40

            These vary a lot,
            so variance is high.

        Practical interpretation in Data Science

            Student marks
                Low variance
                    Most students performed similarly

                High variance
                    There is a big difference between weak and strong students

            Product sales
                Low variance
                    Sales are stable

                High variance
                    Sales change a lot from day to day

            Stock prices
                Low variance
                    Less volatile

                High variance
                    More risky

        Important note about variance

            Variance uses squared differences.
            So the unit becomes squared.

            Example:
                If marks are in points,
                variance is in points squared

            Because of this,
            variance can be harder to interpret directly.

            That is why standard deviation is often preferred in practice.

        Relation between variance and standard deviation

            Standard deviation = square root of variance

            Example:
                If variance = 25
                then standard deviation = 5

            Standard deviation is easier to explain because it is
            in the original unit of the data.

        Why square the differences

            If we do not square the differences,
            positive and negative differences cancel each other.

            Example:
                Data:
                    2, 4, 6

                Mean:
                    4

                Differences:
                    -2, 0, 2

                Sum of differences:
                    -2 + 0 + 2 = 0

            This would wrongly suggest no spread.
            Squaring solves this problem.

        Example with words meaning

            Suppose two classes have the same average mark = 60

            Class A:
                58, 60, 62, 59, 61

            Class B:
                20, 40, 60, 80, 100

            Both have the same center,
            but class B is much more spread out.

            So:
                Class A has low variance
                Class B has high variance

            Interpretation:
                Class A performance is more consistent
                Class B performance is more uneven

        Variance in model evaluation

            In Data Science,
            variance is also used in another sense in machine learning.

            Sometimes we say:
                A model has high variance

            This means:
                The model changes too much with training data
                It may overfit

            Simple idea:
                High variance model learns training data too closely
                and performs poorly on new data

            This is slightly different from simple data spread,
            but the idea of instability is similar.

        When interpreting variance, ask:
            Is the variance small or large?
            Compared to what?
            What is the unit of the original data?
            Does high spread make practical sense?
            Are there outliers causing the variance?

        Summary idea

            Variance helps us understand consistency and spread.
            It is useful,
            but should often be interpreted along with:
                Mean
                Median
                Standard deviation
                Range
                Graphs


    Simple Combined Example

        Data:
            5, 7, 7, 9, 12

        Mean:
            (5 + 7 + 7 + 9 + 12) / 5
            = 40 / 5
            = 8

        Median:
            7

        Mode:
            7

        Range:
            12 - 5 = 7

        Interpretation:
            Center is around 7 to 8
            Data is moderately spread
            7 is the most common value

        If values were:
            5, 7, 7, 9, 50

        Mean:
            (5 + 7 + 7 + 9 + 50) / 5
            = 78 / 5
            = 15.6

        Median:
            7

        Mode:
            7

        Range:
            50 - 5 = 45

        Interpretation:
            Mean increased because of outlier 50
            Median stayed more stable
            Range became much larger
            Spread increased a lot


    Quick Revision

        Centrality measures
            Show the center of data

            Mean
                Arithmetic average

            Median
                Middle value

            Mode
                Most frequent value

        Variability measures
            Show the spread of data

            Range
                Maximum - Minimum

            IQR
                Q3 - Q1

            Variance
                Average squared distance from mean

            Standard deviation
                Square root of variance

        Interpreting variance
            Low variance
                Data values are close to mean

            High variance
                Data values are far from mean

            Variance helps judge consistency and spread

1.8 Correlation Analysis
******************************************
    Correlation analysis is used to study the relationship between two variables.

    Simple meaning:
        It tells us whether two variables move together,
        move in opposite directions,
        or have no clear relationship.

    Example:
        If students who study more hours usually get higher marks,
        then study hours and marks are positively related.

    Why correlation is important in Data Science
        Helps find relationships in data
        Helps select useful variables
        Helps understand patterns before modeling
        Helps identify redundancy between features
        Helps support prediction and decision making

    Common examples
        Study hours and exam marks
        Advertisement spending and sales
        Temperature and ice cream sales
        Rainfall and crop production
        Age and blood pressure

    Important idea
        Correlation does not always mean cause.
        Two variables may move together without one directly causing the other.

    Example:
        Ice cream sales and drowning cases may both increase in summer.
        This does not mean ice cream causes drowning.
        The hidden factor is hot weather.

    General types of correlation

        Positive correlation
            Both variables move in the same direction

            Example:
                More study hours, more marks
                More advertisement, more sales

        Negative correlation
            One variable increases while the other decreases

            Example:
                More absences, lower marks
                Higher price, lower demand

        Zero or weak correlation
            No strong linear relationship is seen

            Example:
                Shoe size and intelligence usually have no meaningful relationship

    Visual idea of correlation

        Positive correlation
            X increases
            Y also increases

        Negative correlation
            X increases
            Y decreases

        No correlation
            Values appear scattered without a clear pattern

    Example with simple data

        Study Hours     Marks
        1               40
        2               50
        3               60
        4               70
        5               80

        Here,
            as study hours increase,
            marks also increase.

        This suggests positive correlation.

    Another example

        Price           Demand
        10              100
        20               80
        30               60
        40               40
        50               20

        Here,
            as price increases,
            demand decreases.

        This suggests negative correlation.


    1.8.1 Correlation Coefficients
******************************************
        A correlation coefficient is a numerical value that measures
        the strength and direction of relationship between two variables.

        Most common symbol:
            r

        Range of correlation coefficient
            -1 to +1

        Meaning of values

            r = +1
                Perfect positive correlation

            r = -1
                Perfect negative correlation

            r = 0
                No linear correlation

            r between 0 and +1
                Positive correlation of varying strength

            r between 0 and -1
                Negative correlation of varying strength

        General interpretation

            +0.90 to +1.00
                Very strong positive correlation

            +0.70 to +0.89
                Strong positive correlation

            +0.40 to +0.69
                Moderate positive correlation

            +0.10 to +0.39
                Weak positive correlation

            0
                No linear correlation

            -0.10 to -0.39
                Weak negative correlation

            -0.40 to -0.69
                Moderate negative correlation

            -0.70 to -0.89
                Strong negative correlation

            -0.90 to -1.00
                Very strong negative correlation

        Important note
            These interpretations are general.
            In real applications, meaning depends on context.

        Pearson correlation coefficient

            Pearson correlation is the most common correlation coefficient.
            It measures linear relationship between two numerical variables.

            It is useful when:
                Data is numerical
                Relationship is approximately linear

        Example of positive Pearson correlation

            Hours Studied     Marks
            2                 45
            4                 55
            6                 65
            8                 75

            Since both increase together,
            correlation will be positive and strong.

        Example of negative Pearson correlation

            Hours of TV       Marks
            1                 80
            2                 70
            3                 60
            4                 50

            Since TV hours increase while marks decrease,
            correlation will be negative.

        Example of no clear correlation

            Shoe Size         Marks
            5                 70
            6                 65
            7                 80
            8                 60
            9                 75

            There is no clear pattern,
            so correlation may be near zero.

        Simple meaning of strength

            Strong correlation
                Points are close to a straight line

            Weak correlation
                Points are more scattered

        Sign of correlation

            Positive sign
                Variables move in same direction

            Negative sign
                Variables move in opposite direction

        Example with words

            Variable 1
                Number of positive words in a review

            Variable 2
                Review rating

            If more positive words usually mean higher rating,
            then there may be positive correlation.

        Why correlation coefficients are useful in Data Science

            Feature selection
                Helps choose variables related to target variable

            Detecting multicollinearity
                If two input variables are highly correlated,
                one may be unnecessary

            Exploratory Data Analysis
                Helps understand relationships before modeling

            Prediction
                Strongly related variables may be useful predictors

        Important caution

            Correlation only measures association,
            not cause and effect.

        Another caution

            Pearson correlation mainly captures linear relationship.

            Example:
                If a relationship is curved,
                Pearson correlation may be low
                even though variables are related.

        Example of this problem

            Suppose:
                As temperature increases from low to medium,
                productivity increases,
                but after very high temperature,
                productivity decreases.

            This is a curved relationship.
            Pearson correlation may not describe it well.

        Rank correlation

            Sometimes we use correlation based on ranks,
            such as Spearman correlation.

            This is helpful when:
                Data is ordinal
                Relationship is monotonic but not exactly linear
                Outliers exist

        Simple difference

            Pearson
                Measures linear correlation

            Spearman
                Measures rank-based relationship

        Quick example of interpretation

            If r = 0.85
                Strong positive relationship

            If r = -0.76
                Strong negative relationship

            If r = 0.12
                Weak positive relationship

            If r = 0
                No linear relationship

        Example in student data

            Attendance     Marks
            50             40
            60             50
            70             58
            80             70
            90             82

            Likely result:
                Positive correlation

            Interpretation:
                Students with higher attendance tend to score more

        Example in business

            Ad Spending     Sales
            1000            12000
            2000            17000
            3000            22000

            Likely result:
                Positive correlation

            Interpretation:
                Higher advertising is associated with higher sales

        Limits of correlation coefficient

            Sensitive to outliers
            Does not prove causation
            May miss nonlinear relationship
            Value can be affected by small sample size

        Outlier example

            Data:
                X = 1, 2, 3, 4, 100
                Y = 2, 4, 6, 8, 1

            The outlier can strongly disturb correlation.
            So data should be checked before interpreting r.


    1.8.2 Autocorrelation
******************************************
        Autocorrelation means correlation of a variable with itself
        over different time intervals.

        Simple meaning:
            It measures whether current values are related
            to past values of the same variable.

        This is especially important in time series data.

        Examples of time series data
            Daily temperature
            Monthly sales
            Stock prices
            Yearly rainfall
            Hourly electricity use

        Example:
            If today's temperature is strongly related to yesterday's temperature,
            then temperature data has autocorrelation.

        Why autocorrelation matters
            Helps understand time dependence
            Helps detect repeating patterns
            Helps in forecasting
            Helps check model assumptions in time series analysis

        Basic idea of lag

            Lag means previous time step.

            Lag 1
                Compare present value with previous value

            Lag 2
                Compare present value with value two steps earlier

            Example:
                Sales data:
                    Day 1    100
                    Day 2    110
                    Day 3    120
                    Day 4    130

                Lag 1 compares:
                    Day 2 with Day 1
                    Day 3 with Day 2
                    Day 4 with Day 3

        Positive autocorrelation

            When high values tend to follow high values,
            and low values tend to follow low values

            Example:
                Temperature:
                    28, 29, 30, 31, 30, 29

            Nearby values are similar,
            so positive autocorrelation is likely.

        Negative autocorrelation

            When high values tend to be followed by low values,
            and low values by high values

            Example:
                10, 20, 10, 20, 10, 20

            This alternating pattern suggests negative autocorrelation.

        Zero autocorrelation

            No clear time-based relationship between nearby values

        Example of autocorrelation in sales

            Monthly sales:
                Jan    1000
                Feb    1050
                Mar    1100
                Apr    1080
                May    1120

            These values are close from one month to the next.
            So there may be positive autocorrelation.

        Example in stock data

            If today's stock price movement depends strongly on yesterday's,
            then stock data may show autocorrelation.

        Example in education

            Weekly attendance:
                Week 1    80
                Week 2    82
                Week 3    81
                Week 4    83

            Attendance in one week may be related to previous week attendance.
            This is time-based self-relationship.

        How autocorrelation is used

            Forecasting
                Future values may be predicted using past values

            Pattern detection
                Helps identify seasonality and trends

            Model checking
                Residual autocorrelation may show model weakness

            Time series analysis
                Important in AR, MA, ARIMA models

        Simple intuitive example

            Suppose a student's daily study hours are:
                2, 2.5, 3, 3.5, 4

            Each day is similar to previous day.
            So study hours show positive autocorrelation.

        Another example

            Machine temperature every hour:
                70, 72, 71, 73, 74

            Nearby readings are related,
            so autocorrelation exists.

        Autocorrelation in residuals

            In model building,
            residuals should often not show strong autocorrelation.

            If residuals are autocorrelated,
            it may mean the model missed an important time pattern.

        Example:
            A sales prediction model leaves errors like:
                +5, +6, +7, +8

            These errors are all moving in a pattern,
            suggesting autocorrelation.

            This means the model may need improvement.

        Difference between correlation and autocorrelation

            Correlation
                Relationship between two different variables

                Example:
                    Study hours and marks

            Autocorrelation
                Relationship between one variable and its past values

                Example:
                    Today's sales and yesterday's sales

        Important in Data Science
            Correlation is useful in general data analysis
            Autocorrelation is especially useful in time-based data

        Positive autocorrelation example with numbers

            Time          Value
            1             10
            2             12
            3             14
            4             16
            5             18

            Values rise smoothly,
            so nearby values are strongly related.

        Negative autocorrelation example with numbers

            Time          Value
            1             10
            2             20
            3             10
            4             20
            5             10

            This alternating pattern suggests negative autocorrelation.

        Why autocorrelation can be a problem

            Some statistical methods assume observations are independent.
            If data has autocorrelation,
            that assumption is violated.

            This can affect:
                Hypothesis testing
                Regression results
                Forecast accuracy

        Real-life meaning

            High autocorrelation
                The past strongly influences the present

            Low autocorrelation
                The present is less related to the past

        Tools for checking autocorrelation

            Autocorrelation plot
            Lag plot
            ACF function
            Durbin-Watson statistic in some regression settings

        Simple interpretation of autocorrelation

            If lag 1 autocorrelation is high positive
                Consecutive observations are similar

            If lag 1 autocorrelation is near zero
                Consecutive observations are not strongly related

            If lag 1 autocorrelation is negative
                Consecutive observations tend to move in opposite directions


    Simple Combined Example

        Example 1
            Study Hours     Marks
            1               40
            2               50
            3               60
            4               70

            Interpretation:
                Positive correlation between study hours and marks

        Example 2
            Day             Sales
            1               100
            2               102
            3               105
            4               103

            Interpretation:
                Sales on one day are related to previous day sales
                This suggests autocorrelation

        So:
            Correlation compares different variables
            Autocorrelation compares one variable across time


    Quick Revision

        Correlation analysis
            Studies relationship between variables

        Correlation coefficient
            Numerical value showing direction and strength of relationship

            +1
                Perfect positive

            0
                No linear correlation

            -1
                Perfect negative

        Correlation does not mean causation

        Autocorrelation
            Correlation of a variable with its own past values

        Useful for
            Time series analysis
            Forecasting
            Pattern detection
            Model checking
