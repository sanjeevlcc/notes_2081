UNIT I OVERVIEW
============================================================

. Simulation and Modeling is a method of studying real-world systems
  using simplified representations called models.

. A real-world system may be simple or complex.

. In many cases, experimenting directly with the real system is costly,
  risky, slow, or impossible.

. Therefore, we first create a model of the system.

. Then we perform simulation on that model.

. Simulation helps us understand how the system behaves under different
  conditions.

. It also helps in prediction, decision making, planning, and system
  improvement.

------------------------------------------------------------
Simple Example
------------------------------------------------------------

. Suppose a hospital has many patients waiting at the registration
  counter.

. Instead of directly changing the real hospital system, we can create
  a model of the patient arrival and service process.

. Then we can simulate different situations, such as:
  . What happens if one more staff is added?
  . What happens if patient arrival increases?
  . What happens if service time is reduced?

. This helps hospital management make better decisions.

1.1 SYSTEM, MODEL, AND SIMULATION
============================================================

------------------------------------------------------------
1. SYSTEM
------------------------------------------------------------

. A system is a collection of interrelated components that work together
  to achieve a common objective.

. A system has input, process, output, boundary, environment, and
  feedback.

. A system may be physical, abstract, natural, artificial, simple, or
  complex.

------------------------------------------------------------
Definition
------------------------------------------------------------

. A system is an organized group of components that interact with each
  other to perform a specific function or achieve a common goal.







------------------------------------------------------------
Main Components of a System
------------------------------------------------------------

. Input:
  Data, material, energy, or information entering the system.

. Process:
  The operation or activity that converts input into output.

. Output:
  The result produced by the system.

. Boundary:
  The limit that separates the system from its environment.

. Environment:
  Everything outside the system that can affect the system.

. Feedback:
  Information about output that is returned to control or improve the
  system.



------------------------------------------------------------
Simple Diagram of a System
------------------------------------------------------------

          +-------------+       +-------------+       +-------------+
          |   INPUT     | ----> |   PROCESS   | ----> |   OUTPUT    |
          +-------------+       +-------------+       +-------------+
                                      ^
                                      |
                                +-----------+
                                | FEEDBACK  |
                                +-----------+





------------------------------------------------------------
Examples of Systems
------------------------------------------------------------

. Computer system:
  . Input: Keyboard, mouse, data
  . Process: CPU processing
  . Output: Monitor display, printout

. Hospital system:
  . Input: Patients, medical records
  . Process: Diagnosis and treatment
  . Output: Treated patients

. Banking system:
  . Input: Customers, money, transaction request
  . Process: Deposit, withdrawal, loan processing
  . Output: Completed transaction

. Coffee production system:
  . Input: Coffee cherry or parchment
  . Process: Hulling, grading, roasting, packaging
  . Output: Roasted coffee beans






------------------------------------------------------------
Types of System Based on Nature
------------------------------------------------------------

. Physical system:
  A system that exists physically.

  Example:
  . Computer hardware
  . Road traffic system
  . Hospital building

. Abstract system:
  A conceptual or logical system.

  Example:
  . Mathematical model
  . Economic model
  . Software design model

. Natural system:
  A system created by nature.

  Example:
  . Solar system
  . Human body
  . Weather system

. Artificial system:
  A system created by humans.

  Example:
  . Computer network
  . Banking system
  . Transportation system









------------------------------------------------------------
2. MODEL
------------------------------------------------------------

. A model is a simplified representation of a real system.

. A model does not include every detail of the real system.

. It includes only important features needed for study and analysis.

------------------------------------------------------------
Definition
------------------------------------------------------------

. A model is a simplified representation of a real system used to
  understand, analyze, predict, or control the behavior of that system.

------------------------------------------------------------
Why Model is Needed
------------------------------------------------------------

. Real systems may be too complex.

. Real experiments may be expensive.

. Real experiments may be dangerous.

. Real experiments may take too much time.

. Some systems may not yet exist.

. Models help us test ideas before implementation.

------------------------------------------------------------
Examples of Models
------------------------------------------------------------

. Map is a model of a city.

. Globe is a model of the earth.

. Circuit diagram is a model of an electrical system.

. Flowchart is a model of a program logic.

. Mathematical equation is a model of a real process.

. Queue table is a model of a bank waiting line.





------------------------------------------------------------
Simple Example of Model
------------------------------------------------------------

Real system:

. A coffee shop receives customers and serves coffee.

Important variables:

. Customer arrival rate
. Service time
. Number of staff
. Waiting time
. Queue length

Model:

. A table or computer program that represents customer arrival,
  service time, and waiting time.





------------------------------------------------------------
3. SIMULATION
------------------------------------------------------------

. Simulation is the imitation of the operation of a real-world process
  or system over time.

. Simulation uses a model to study the behavior of the real system.

. It is like performing experiments on a model instead of the actual
  system.

------------------------------------------------------------
Definition
------------------------------------------------------------

. Simulation is the process of designing a model of a real system and
  conducting experiments with that model to understand system behavior
  or evaluate different strategies.

------------------------------------------------------------
Why Simulation is Needed
------------------------------------------------------------

. To study complex systems.

. To test a system before building it.

. To reduce cost.

. To reduce risk.

. To save time.

. To compare alternatives.

. To predict future behavior.

. To support decision making.

------------------------------------------------------------
Examples of Simulation
------------------------------------------------------------

. Simulating traffic flow at a busy junction.

. Simulating patient waiting time in a hospital.

. Simulating customer queue in a bank.

. Simulating network traffic in a computer network.

. Simulating coffee roasting capacity and daily sales.

. Simulating weather conditions.

. Simulating software development process.

------------------------------------------------------------
Relationship Between System, Model, and Simulation
------------------------------------------------------------

. A system is the real object or process to be studied.

. A model is a simplified representation of the system.

. Simulation is the process of running experiments on the model.

------------------------------------------------------------
Example
------------------------------------------------------------

. System:
  Real bank counter.

. Model:
  Queue model of customer arrival and service time.

. Simulation:
  Running the queue model to find average waiting time.

1.2 DISCRETE AND CONTINUOUS SYSTEMS
============================================================

. Systems can be classified based on how their state changes with time.

. The two important types are:
  . Discrete system
  . Continuous system

------------------------------------------------------------
1. DISCRETE SYSTEM
------------------------------------------------------------

. A discrete system changes its state at separate points in time.

. The system state changes only when an event occurs.

. Between two events, the system remains unchanged.

------------------------------------------------------------
Definition
------------------------------------------------------------

. A discrete system is a system in which the state variables change only
  at countable points in time.

------------------------------------------------------------
Examples of Discrete Systems
------------------------------------------------------------

. Bank queue system.

. Ticket counter system.

. Hospital registration system.

. Inventory stock system.

. Computer network packet arrival system.

. Call center system.

------------------------------------------------------------
Example: Bank Queue
------------------------------------------------------------

. Events:
  . Customer arrives.
  . Customer starts service.
  . Customer completes service.
  . Customer leaves the system.

. State variables:
  . Number of customers in queue.
  . Server status: busy or idle.
  . Waiting time.

. The system changes only when an event happens.

------------------------------------------------------------
Discrete System Timeline
------------------------------------------------------------

Time:     0      2      5      9      12
          |------|------|------|------|
Event:    Start  Arrival Service Arrival Service End

. System state changes at time 2, 5, 9, and 12.

------------------------------------------------------------
2. CONTINUOUS SYSTEM
------------------------------------------------------------

. A continuous system changes its state continuously over time.

. The system variables change at every moment.

------------------------------------------------------------
Definition
------------------------------------------------------------

. A continuous system is a system in which the state variables change
  continuously with respect to time.

------------------------------------------------------------
Examples of Continuous Systems
------------------------------------------------------------

. Water level in a tank.

. Temperature of a room.

. Speed of a moving vehicle.

. Population growth.

. Disease spread.

. Chemical reaction.

. Coffee roasting temperature change.

------------------------------------------------------------
Example: Water Tank
------------------------------------------------------------

. Water enters the tank continuously.

. Water leaves the tank continuously.

. The water level changes continuously over time.

. State variable:
  . Water level.

------------------------------------------------------------
Continuous System Timeline
------------------------------------------------------------

Time:   0 ---- 1 ---- 2 ---- 3 ---- 4 ---- 5

Value:  Changes smoothly at every moment

------------------------------------------------------------
Comparison Between Discrete and Continuous Systems
------------------------------------------------------------

. Basis:
  Discrete System:
  Changes at specific points in time.

  Continuous System:
  Changes continuously over time.

------------------------------------------------------------

. State change:
  Discrete System:
  Event-based.

  Continuous System:
  Time-based.

------------------------------------------------------------

. Examples:
  Discrete System:
  Bank queue, ticket counter, inventory.

  Continuous System:
  Water level, temperature, speed.

------------------------------------------------------------

. Mathematical form:
  Discrete System:
  Difference equations, event tables.

  Continuous System:
  Differential equations.

------------------------------------------------------------

. Simulation type:
  Discrete System:
  Discrete event simulation.

  Continuous System:
  Continuous simulation.

1.3 MODEL OF A SYSTEM
============================================================

. A model of a system is a representation of the real system.

. It contains important system components, variables, relationships,
  assumptions, and rules.

. The purpose of a model is to understand and analyze system behavior.

------------------------------------------------------------
Elements of a System Model
------------------------------------------------------------

. Entities:
  Objects or components in the system.

. Attributes:
  Properties of entities.

. Activities:
  Actions or processes that occur in the system.

. Events:
  Occurrences that change the state of the system.

. State variables:
  Variables that describe the current condition of the system.

. Parameters:
  Fixed values used in the model.

. Relationships:
  Connections between variables.

. Assumptions:
  Simplifications made while building the model.

------------------------------------------------------------
Example: Bank Queue Model
------------------------------------------------------------

. Entity:
  Customer

. Attribute:
  Arrival time, service time

. Activity:
  Waiting, service

. Event:
  Customer arrival, service completion

. State variable:
  Queue length, server status

. Parameter:
  Average arrival rate, average service rate

. Output:
  Average waiting time, server utilization

------------------------------------------------------------
Example: Coffee Shop Model
------------------------------------------------------------

. Entity:
  Customer, staff, coffee machine

. Attribute:
  Order size, service time, price

. Activity:
  Taking order, preparing coffee, payment

. Event:
  Customer arrives, order completed

. State variable:
  Number of customers waiting

. Output:
  Daily sales, waiting time, profit

------------------------------------------------------------
Steps to Build a System Model
------------------------------------------------------------

. Step 1:
  Identify the real system.

. Step 2:
  Define the objective of the model.

. Step 3:
  Identify system boundary.

. Step 4:
  Identify important variables.

. Step 5:
  Identify input and output.

. Step 6:
  Define assumptions.

. Step 7:
  Establish relationships among variables.

. Step 8:
  Test and validate the model.

============================================================
1.4 TYPES OF MODELS
============================================================

. Models can be classified in different ways depending on their nature,
  structure, purpose, and representation.

============================================================
A. PHYSICAL MODEL
============================================================

. A physical model is a smaller or larger physical representation of a
  real system.

. It can be touched and seen.

Examples:

. Globe as a model of the earth.

. Toy car as a model of a real car.

. Building model before construction.

. Bridge model for engineering test.

Advantages:

. Easy to understand.

. Good for demonstration.

Limitations:

. May be expensive.

. Cannot represent all details.

============================================================
B. MATHEMATICAL MODEL
============================================================

. A mathematical model represents a system using equations and formulas.

Examples:

. Profit = Revenue - Cost

. Distance = Speed x Time

. Area of circle = πr²

. Population growth model.

Advantages:

. Precise.

. Useful for calculation.

. Can be solved analytically or numerically.

Limitations:

. Requires mathematical knowledge.

. May oversimplify real systems.

============================================================
C. LOGICAL MODEL
============================================================

. A logical model represents the logical flow or structure of a system.

Examples:

. Flowchart.

. Algorithm.

. Decision table.

. Entity relationship diagram.

. Data flow diagram.

Advantages:

. Shows process clearly.

. Useful in software and system design.

Limitations:

. Does not always show numerical behavior.

============================================================
D. GRAPHICAL MODEL
============================================================

. A graphical model represents a system using diagrams, graphs, or
  charts.

Examples:

. Network diagram.

. Flow diagram.

. Bar chart.

. Line graph.

. UML diagram.

Advantages:

. Easy to visualize.

. Good for presentation.

Limitations:

. May not provide exact calculations.

============================================================
E. STATIC MODEL
============================================================

. A static model represents a system at a particular point in time.

. Time is not an important factor.

Examples:

. Balance sheet of a company.

. Map of a city.

. Structural diagram of a building.

. One-time profit calculation.

============================================================
F. DYNAMIC MODEL
============================================================

. A dynamic model represents a system that changes over time.

. Time is an important factor.

Examples:

. Traffic flow during a day.

. Population growth over years.

. Queue length over time.

. Temperature change during heating or cooling.

============================================================
G. DETERMINISTIC MODEL
============================================================

. A deterministic model does not include randomness.

. Same input always gives the same output.

Example:

. If a machine produces 10 items per hour, then in 8 hours:

  . Total production = 10 x 8 = 80 items

. The result is always fixed.

============================================================
H. STOCHASTIC MODEL
============================================================

. A stochastic model includes randomness or uncertainty.

. Same input may produce different outputs in different runs.

Example:

. Customer arrival in a bank.

. Daily sales in a coffee shop.

. Weather prediction.

. Stock price movement.

============================================================
I. DISCRETE MODEL
============================================================

. A discrete model represents a system where state changes happen at
  separate points in time.

Example:

. Queue model of a bank.

. Inventory model.

. Ticket booking model.

============================================================
J. CONTINUOUS MODEL
============================================================

. A continuous model represents a system where state variables change
  continuously over time.

Example:

. Water level model.

. Temperature model.

. Speed model.

. Chemical reaction model.

============================================================
Summary of Types of Models
============================================================

. Physical Model:
  Real physical representation.

. Mathematical Model:
  Equations and formulas.

. Logical Model:
  Process logic and decision flow.

. Graphical Model:
  Diagram or graph representation.

. Static Model:
  Time-independent model.

. Dynamic Model:
  Time-dependent model.

. Deterministic Model:
  No randomness.

. Stochastic Model:
  Includes randomness.

. Discrete Model:
  Event-based model.

. Continuous Model:
  Continuous time-based model.

1.5 COMPUTER SIMULATION
============================================================

. Computer simulation is the use of computer programs to imitate the
  behavior of a real-world system.

. It is one of the most widely used simulation techniques.

. It helps to study systems that are difficult to analyze manually or
  mathematically.

------------------------------------------------------------
Definition
------------------------------------------------------------

. Computer simulation is the process of designing a model of a real
  system and using a computer program to run experiments on that model.

------------------------------------------------------------
Why Computer Simulation is Used
------------------------------------------------------------

. Computers can process large amounts of data quickly.

. Computers can repeat experiments many times.

. Computers reduce manual calculation errors.

. Complex systems can be simulated easily.

. Results can be shown using tables, charts, and graphs.

------------------------------------------------------------
Examples of Computer Simulation
------------------------------------------------------------

. Simulating network traffic.

. Simulating bank queue.

. Simulating weather conditions.

. Simulating hospital patient flow.

. Simulating stock market risk.

. Simulating coffee business profit.

. Simulating software development process.

------------------------------------------------------------
Common Tools for Computer Simulation
------------------------------------------------------------

. Python

. C / C++

. Java

. MATLAB

. R

. Excel

. Simulink

. Arena

. AnyLogic

. GPSS

. Cisco Packet Tracer

------------------------------------------------------------
Basic Computer Simulation Process
------------------------------------------------------------

. Step 1:
  Define the problem.

. Step 2:
  Build the model.

. Step 3:
  Collect input data.

. Step 4:
  Write the computer program.

. Step 5:
  Run the simulation.

. Step 6:
  Analyze output.

. Step 7:
  Validate the result.

. Step 8:
  Make decision.

------------------------------------------------------------
Simple Example: Computer Simulation of Queue
------------------------------------------------------------

Problem:

. A bank wants to know average customer waiting time.

Input data:

. Customer arrival time.

. Service time.

. Number of counters.

Simulation output:

. Average waiting time.

. Maximum queue length.

. Counter idle time.

. Counter utilization.

Decision:

. If waiting time is high, add another counter.

1.6 MODEL DEVELOPMENT LIFE CYCLE
============================================================

. Model development life cycle is the systematic process of developing,
  testing, validating, using, and improving a model.

. It ensures that the model is correct, reliable, and useful.

------------------------------------------------------------
Stages of Model Development Life Cycle
------------------------------------------------------------

. 1. Problem identification

. 2. Objective definition

. 3. System study

. 4. Conceptual model development

. 5. Data collection

. 6. Model formulation

. 7. Model programming

. 8. Verification

. 9. Validation

. 10. Experimentation

. 11. Output analysis

. 12. Documentation

. 13. Implementation

. 14. Maintenance and improvement

============================================================
1. PROBLEM IDENTIFICATION
============================================================

. Identify the real problem clearly.

. Understand what needs to be solved.

Example:

. Patients are waiting too long at a hospital registration counter.

============================================================
2. OBJECTIVE DEFINITION
============================================================

. Define the purpose of the model.

. Decide what output is expected.

Example:

. Objective is to reduce patient waiting time.

============================================================
3. SYSTEM STUDY
============================================================

. Study the real system carefully.

. Identify system components, input, output, process, and constraints.

Example:

. Study arrival rate, service time, number of staff, and working hours.

============================================================
4. CONCEPTUAL MODEL DEVELOPMENT
============================================================

. Prepare a simple logical representation of the system.

. It may include diagrams, flowcharts, and assumptions.

Example:

. Patient arrives -> waits in queue -> gets service -> leaves.

============================================================
5. DATA COLLECTION
============================================================

. Collect real data from the system.

Example:

. Patient arrival time.

. Service time.

. Number of patients per hour.

. Staff working time.

============================================================
6. MODEL FORMULATION
============================================================

. Convert conceptual model into mathematical or logical form.

Example:

. Waiting time = Service start time - Arrival time.

. Time in system = Departure time - Arrival time.

============================================================
7. MODEL PROGRAMMING
============================================================

. Implement the model using computer software or programming language.

Example:

. Use Excel, Python, C, Java, MATLAB, or simulation software.

============================================================
8. VERIFICATION
============================================================

. Verification checks whether the model has been built correctly.

. It answers the question:

  . Are we building the model right?

Example:

. Check whether the program logic and formulas are correct.

============================================================
9. VALIDATION
============================================================

. Validation checks whether the model represents the real system
  correctly.

. It answers the question:

  . Are we building the right model?

Example:

. Compare simulation result with actual hospital waiting time.

============================================================
10. EXPERIMENTATION
============================================================

. Run the model under different conditions.

Example:

. Experiment 1:
  One staff.

. Experiment 2:
  Two staff.

. Experiment 3:
  Three staff.

============================================================
11. OUTPUT ANALYSIS
============================================================

. Analyze the results obtained from simulation.

Example:

. Average waiting time.

. Queue length.

. Server utilization.

. Cost.

. Profit.

============================================================
12. DOCUMENTATION
============================================================

. Prepare complete documentation of the model.

Documentation includes:

. Problem statement.

. Assumptions.

. Data used.

. Model logic.

. Program description.

. Results.

. Limitations.

. Recommendations.

============================================================
13. IMPLEMENTATION
============================================================

. Apply the best solution to the real system.

Example:

. If simulation shows two staff are enough, hospital management may add
  one more staff.

============================================================
14. MAINTENANCE AND IMPROVEMENT
============================================================

. After implementation, the model may need updates.

. New data and changing conditions should be added.

Example:

. If patient arrival increases in future, the model should be updated.

1.7 ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES OF SIMULATION
============================================================

============================================================
ADVANTAGES OF SIMULATION
============================================================

. 1. Safe experimentation

  . Simulation allows testing without disturbing the real system.

  Example:
  . Testing traffic signal timing without changing actual road signals.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 2. Cost saving

  . It is cheaper than real-world experimentation.

  Example:
  . Testing factory layout using simulation before construction.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 3. Time saving

  . Long-term behavior can be studied in short time.

  Example:
  . Simulating 10 years of population growth in a few seconds.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 4. Risk reduction

  . Dangerous situations can be tested safely.

  Example:
  . Flight training using flight simulator.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 5. What-if analysis

  . Different alternatives can be tested.

  Example:
  . What happens if one more counter is added in a bank?

------------------------------------------------------------

. 6. Useful for complex systems

  . Simulation can handle systems that are difficult to solve
    mathematically.

  Example:
  . Weather forecasting and traffic flow.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 7. Better decision making

  . Simulation provides information for selecting the best alternative.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 8. Training purpose

  . Simulation provides realistic practice environments.

  Example:
  . Medical surgery simulator, flight simulator, driving simulator.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 9. Supports prediction

  . Future behavior of a system can be predicted.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 10. Flexible

  . Model parameters can be changed easily.

============================================================
DISADVANTAGES OF SIMULATION
============================================================

. 1. Approximate result

  . Simulation usually gives approximate results, not exact answers.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 2. Model building is difficult

  . Developing a good model requires skill and knowledge.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 3. Requires valid data

  . Poor input data gives poor output.

  . This is called garbage in, garbage out.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 4. Time-consuming development

  . Large simulation models may take long time to develop.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 5. Expensive software

  . Some simulation tools may be costly.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 6. Requires technical knowledge

  . Programming, statistics, mathematics, and system knowledge may be
    required.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 7. Difficult validation

  . It may be difficult to prove that the model truly represents the
    real system.

------------------------------------------------------------

. 8. Misinterpretation of results

  . Wrong interpretation can lead to wrong decision.

1.9 STEPS IN SIMULATION AND MODELING
============================================================

. Simulation and modeling should be done systematically.

. The main steps are:

  . Problem formulation
  . Model construction
  . Data collection
  . Model programming
  . Validation
  . Design of experiment
  . Simulation run and analysis
  . Documentation
  . Implementation

============================================================
STEP 1: PROBLEM FORMULATION
============================================================

. Problem formulation means clearly defining the problem to be solved.

. It is the first and most important step.

Questions to ask:

. What is the problem?

. Why is simulation needed?

. What system is being studied?

. What are the objectives?

. What outputs are required?

Example:

. A bank wants to reduce customer waiting time.

Problem statement:

. Customers are waiting too long at the bank counter.

Objective:

. Find the required number of counters to reduce waiting time.

============================================================
STEP 2: MODEL CONSTRUCTION
============================================================

. Model construction means building a representation of the real system.

. The model may be mathematical, logical, graphical, or computer-based.

Activities:

. Identify system components.

. Define system boundary.

. Identify variables.

. Define assumptions.

. Establish relationships among variables.

Example:

. Bank queue model:

  . Customer arrival.
  . Queue.
  . Service counter.
  . Customer departure.

Simple model:

. Customer arrives -> waits -> gets service -> leaves.

============================================================
STEP 3: DATA COLLECTION
============================================================

. Data collection means collecting required input data for the model.

Types of data:

. Arrival time.

. Service time.

. Processing time.

. Failure rate.

. Cost.

. Demand.

. Production rate.

. Probability values.

Example:

. For bank queue simulation:

  . Number of customers per hour.
  . Average service time.
  . Number of counters.
  . Working hours.

Importance:

. Accurate data produces reliable simulation results.

. Wrong data gives wrong result.

============================================================
STEP 4: MODEL PROGRAMMING
============================================================

. Model programming means converting the model into a computer program.

. A programming language or simulation software may be used.

Common tools:

. Python

. C

. C++

. Java

. MATLAB

. Excel

. R

. Arena

. AnyLogic

Example:

. Write a Python program to calculate customer waiting time.

============================================================
STEP 5: VALIDATION
============================================================

. Validation checks whether the model represents the real system
  correctly.

. It ensures that the model is useful and realistic.

Question:

. Are we building the right model?

Validation methods:

. Compare model output with real system output.

. Ask experts to check the model.

. Test assumptions.

. Use historical data.

Example:

. If actual average waiting time in bank is 12 minutes and simulation
  also gives around 12 minutes, the model may be valid.

============================================================
STEP 6: DESIGN OF EXPERIMENT
============================================================

. Design of experiment means planning how simulation experiments will be
  performed.

. It defines what input values will be changed and what output will be
  measured.

Questions:

. How many simulation runs will be performed?

. What variables will be changed?

. What scenarios will be tested?

. What performance measures will be recorded?

Example:

. Bank queue experiment:

  . Scenario 1: One counter.
  . Scenario 2: Two counters.
  . Scenario 3: Three counters.

Performance measures:

. Average waiting time.

. Maximum queue length.

. Counter utilization.

============================================================
STEP 7: SIMULATION RUN AND ANALYSIS
============================================================

. Simulation run means executing the model.

. Analysis means studying the output generated by the simulation.

Outputs may include:

. Average waiting time.

. Total profit.

. Production quantity.

. Queue length.

. Resource utilization.

. System cost.

. Error percentage.

Example:

. Result from bank simulation:

  . One counter: average waiting time = 20 minutes.
  . Two counters: average waiting time = 8 minutes.
  . Three counters: average waiting time = 3 minutes.

Analysis:

. Two counters may be better because waiting time is acceptable and cost
  is lower than three counters.

. Problem description.

. Model structure.

. Experiment design.

STEP 9: IMPLEMENTATION
============================================================

. Implementation means applying the best solution in the real system.

. After analyzing results, the selected decision is used practically.

Example:

. If simulation shows that two counters reduce waiting time effectively,
  the bank may add one extra counter.

. If coffee roasting simulation shows machine capacity is low, the
  company may increase working hours or buy another roaster.

Problem:

. Customers wait too long in a bank.

Objective:

. Find whether one more counter is needed.

Model variables:

. Customer arrival time.

. Service time.

. Number of counters.

. Waiting time.

Data:

. Average customer arrival = 20 customers per hour.

. Average service time = 4 minutes.

Experiments:

. Scenario 1:
  One counter.

. Scenario 2:
  Two counters.

. Scenario 3:
  Three counters.

Possible results:

. One counter:
  Average waiting time = 25 minutes.

. Two counters:
  Average waiting time = 8 minutes.

. Three counters:
  Average waiting time = 3 minutes.

Decision:

. Two counters may be best because waiting time becomes acceptable and
  cost is reasonable.

============================================================
FULL EXAMPLE: COFFEE ROASTING SIMULATION
============================================================

Problem:

. A coffee business wants to estimate production capacity.

Given:

. Roaster capacity = 2 kg per hour.

. Working hours per day = 8 hours.

. Working days per month = 25 days.

Model:

. Daily production = Capacity per hour x Working hours.

. Monthly production = Daily production x Working days.

Calculation:

. Daily production = 2 x 8 = 16 kg.

. Monthly production = 16 x 25 = 400 kg.

Simulation question:

. What if monthly demand is 500 kg?

Analysis:

. Current monthly capacity = 400 kg.

. Demand = 500 kg.

. Shortage = 500 - 400 = 100 kg.

Decision:

. Increase working hours, improve efficiency, or buy another roaster.
